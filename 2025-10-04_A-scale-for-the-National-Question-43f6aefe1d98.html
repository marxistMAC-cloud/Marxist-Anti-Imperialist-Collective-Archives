BAUER AND STALIN

Marxists in their work, rarely speak about the national question, and the few times they speak, they mostly speak of it in negative terms, i.e, “ since nations do exist, what we do to make their existence weaker?”. From the early 1900s, Marxists came to the truth of the weight of the importance of the national question for the world’s labouring masses. The line of essential “anti-nationalism” that was spearheaded by plenty of socialists in europe, came to a halt after they clashed with the reality of the nation.

Marxists were some of the first to see this reality and take what was essentially a “pro-nationalist” position, and also the first to combat nationalism at the same time. This is because the Marxist movement at the time was not a monolithic movement, and plenty of internal ideological conflicts were present. The revolution after all had not yet come, and there was no “centre” to gather all people who were operating under the Marxist umbrella and give them a “monolithic” set of lines. Thus, the Marxists came to the “national question” through the arising of nationalism (national consciousness) among’s the masses who had broken from the pre-modern decentralized society of the village (where the whole centre of the world is the village and one’s own family, all being protected from the feudal lord who was god’s chosen ruler) and were now coupled in centralized citycentres.

Not only the political unification of existing nations took place, but the definition of nations took shape in a crude form in the real world through the reality and the objective existance of the nation; the people of France, Italy, e.t.c, all came in the cities and found out that they did not speak the same language. In short, they werent one people, one nation. There was no scientific idea of the nation back then, so this inner feeling that “Italians”, “Ottomans”, e.t.c werent really one nation took (and even today, still takes), crude and primitive forms. Only the Marxists could create a theory that would break with the bourgeoisie notion of state=nation, and give a a scientific view of what is in fact, a nation, and why there was internal national conflict among’s the “Ottomans”, “Italians” e.t.c.

But one things becomes clear: if one wants to have a proper line on the national question, they first need to understand what is a nation to begin with. Likewise, if one wants to destroy capitalism, one needs to understand what capitalism is, same with the state, and same with every single thing. Else, this one will almost always be on the wrong, and the few times he will be on the right, it will be mostly from a combination of instinct and luck. This moment, the moment of the scientific explanation of what is a nation from the marxists, came in late 19st century to early 20st century, when Europe was still largely dominated by multinational formations.

At first, Otto Bauer came to give his own Marxist definition of what is a nation from his book “The question of nationalities and social democracy”.

We cannot quote the whole book, but the central thesis of Bauer can be understood from the following passage in our humble opinion: Is the nation a community of persons of common descent? Surely the Italians are descended from Etruscans, Romans, Celts, Germanic tribes, Greeks, and Saracens, the presentday French from Gauls, Romans, Britons, and Germanic tribes, the present-day Germans from Germanic tribes, Celts, and Slavs.

Or is it the community of language that unites human beings as a nation? Surely the English and Irish, the Danes and Norwegians, the Serbs and Croats, although in each case both speak the same language, do not therefore constitute one people; the Jews do not have a common language and yet they are a nation.

Is it, then, the consciousness of a common bond that unites the nation? Should the Tyrolean peasant not be regarded as a German because he has never become conscious of his common bond with East Prussians, Pomeranians, Thuringians, and Alsatians? And furthermore, what is the German conscious of when he thinks of his “Germanness”? What makes him a member of the German nation, what connects him with other Germans? Surely an objective feature of this common bond must first be present before one can become conscious of it.

A systematic approach to the question of the nation must begin with a conception of national character. If we take any German to a foreign country, if we place him, say, among the English, he will immediately be conscious of the fact that he is among people different from himself, people with a different way of thinking and feeling, people whose reactions to the same external stimuli are quite different from those he finds in his usual German environ ment. For the moment, we will call the complex of physical and intellectual characteristics that distinguishes one nation from another its national character. Over and above these differences, all peoples share certain characteristics that identify us as human beings. On the other hand, classes, professions, and individuals within each nation exhibit distinctive qualities, particular features that distinguish them from one another. But the fact remains that the average German is different from the average Englishman, although they may well have much in common as human beings and as members of the same class or profession, and that the English share with one another a range of character istics, however much they are distinguished from one another by individual and social differences. What is important here are three things: For Bauer, a) common descent is not what defines a nation, b) neither is languange, c) Neither is land. What defines a nation? It is the following according to Bauer:

A nation is an aggregate of people bound into a community of character by a common destiny.
