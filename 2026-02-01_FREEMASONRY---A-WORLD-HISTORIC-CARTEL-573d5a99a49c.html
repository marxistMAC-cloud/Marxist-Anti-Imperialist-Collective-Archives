I. A BRIEF HISTORY OF FREEMASONRY

ACCORDING TO THE NON-MASONS

Freemasonry: the teachings and practices of the secret fraternal order of Free and Accepted Masons, the largest worldwide secret society.

— Encyclopedia Britannica

The Freemasons are something of an enigma. Much of their early history is up to speculation, and this speculation must be drawn from a series of recovered Masonic manuscripts and lodge documents from the late Medieval era and the Renaissance. These documents generally describe two things: the Masonic rules, principles, procedures, etc., many of which the Masons still follow today; and a sort of “Masonic lore,” a record of history as the Masons themselves believe it happened, and with a surprising level of consistency between one another. Collectively, these are referred to as the “Old Charges”, and constitute the basis of modern Freemasonry.

Before we delve into the Old Charges, we should attempt to make a summation of the Masonic history from the non-Masonic perspective. Excluding the Masons as a source, one is not left with much: thus, this summation will be quite brief.

Throughout the 13th and 14th centuries, there are mentions of “freemasons”, stoneworkers, in various royal and church statutes detailing the conditions of their employment. The earliest known reference to masons in this way is the Sculptores Lapidum Liberorum (Sculptors of Free Stone), a set of statutes dated to 1212 in London. In 1425 the English Parliament passed a decree banning meetings of stonemasons, suggesting they had begun to exist as an organization by then; apparently in response to this is the earliest of the known Old Charges, the Regius Poem, written sometime in the years following Parliament’s statute. Thus, the modern Freemasons may be definitively said to have first existed in the first half of the 15th century, if not before then.

The oldest proven Freemason lodge to exist is that of Mary’s Chapel, a lodge in Scotland. It possesses a set of minutes from 1599, putting it a little over a hundred years after the Regius Poem. However, there is also the Lodge Mother Kilwinning, another Scottish lodge which cites the Second Schaw Statutes written by the prominent Freemason and construction master for King James VI, William Schaw.

Since the statutes were written in 1599, the Kilwinning Lodge most likely existed prior (and therefore before Mary’s Chapel). According to the Lodge itself, they were founded in the 1100s; if this is true, Masonry is at least nine centuries old.

In 1717, the Premier Grand Lodge of England was founded when the four major lodges in London united under a common organization. By this time, there were already at least a couple lodges in France. A few years later, the Scottish Masonic scholar Thomas Dunckerley was born. Dunckerley is important: he would go on to become a close friend of King George III, who fabricated evidence of a family tie between Dunckerley and George II in order to appropriate Crown funds for Dunckerley’s Masonic studies. Dunckerley further united the lodges of England and Scotland, and in the 1760s, he would depart to Canada and spread Freemasonry there as well, uniting the Canadian Lodges as Grand Master.

In Britain, Freemasonry appeared to find quite a niche among royalty: Kings William IV, Edward VII, Edward VIII, George III, George VI, and James VI were all Freemasons, and the Regius Manuscript was donated to the Royal Library of London by George II. However, Freemasonry started not with the nobility, but with the urban small-scale producers, the stonemasons. Masonry remained in cahoots with royalty only for as long as they benefited from it: with the rise of liberalism and burgher nationalism in the 20th century, Freemasonry would form the nucleus of the various secret clubs and societies which were so often utilized by the liberal intelligentsia in the overthrow of monarchism, from France to the Philippines.

In America, the liberal revolution was carried out by the already noted Washington and Franklin, as well as Monroe, Jackson, Revere, Adams, Hancock… All of these men were Freemasons. On top of this, something like 41% of Continental generals during the Revolution were Masons too, 16% of the signatories of the Declaration of Independence, and 64% of the signatories of the Constitution. And it was not just a distant fondness. In a letter to a Massachusetts Masonic lodge in 1793 (during his Presidency!) which is now preserved by Congress, the first President of the United States, George Washington, said:

To understand what Masonry is today, one must look at its transition from “Operative” to “Speculative” Masonry. In the late 17th century, the lodges began admitting “Accepted Masons” — men who were not actual stoneworkers but were intellectuals, philosophers, and aristocrats. The Square became a symbol of morality (“acting on the square”); the Compasses a symbol of self-restraint; and the Level a symbol of equality. This “Speculative” Masonry became the primary vehicle for the Enlightenment. Within the secrecy of the lodge, the rigid hierarchies of feudal Europe were temporarily suspended. A duke and a merchant could meet as “Brothers” on the “Level.”

This environment was the laboratory for the liberal democratic ideals that would eventually trigger the American and French Revolutions. THE SOCIETY OF THE CINCINNATI: SECRET AMERICAN NOBLES

It is a group which is not strictly Masonic, but it is so closely tied to it that it may as well be. The Society of the Cincinnati was founded in 1783 by the officers of the Continental Army and Navy who had served in the Revolutionary War. It was named after Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus, a Roman dictator who, according to legend, left his plow to lead the Roman army to victory and then immediately returned to his farm, refusing any political power.

The Society was intended to be a way for the officers to maintain their bonds of friendship and to provide for the widows and orphans of those who had died in the war. However, it was immediately met with intense criticism from those who saw it as an attempt to establish a hereditary aristocracy in the new Republic. This was because membership in the Society was hereditary — passed down from the eldest son to the eldest son, in perpetuity.

Thomas Jefferson, Samuel Adams, and Benjamin Franklin (who was himself a Mason, yet criticized the Society’s hereditary nature) all spoke out against it. Jefferson referred to it as an “order of chivalry” that was inconsistent with the principles of a republic. Despite this, the Society flourished. George Washington was its first President-General, and Alexander Hamilton was its second.

The tie to Masonry is nearly universal within the group; at its founding, the vast majority of its members were active Freemasons. The Society of the Cincinnati represents the “American Nobility” that Masonry helped foster — a class of men who, while speaking the language of “liberty,” viewed themselves as the natural, enlightened rulers of the continent. Even today, the Society maintains its headquarters at Anderson House in Washington D.C., continuing to serve as a private club for the descendants of the Revolutionary officer class.
