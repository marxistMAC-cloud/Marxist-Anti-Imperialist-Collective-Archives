Why Sakai matters

An important intellectual, at best ignored, at worst mocked, by the entirety of Western communist movement, is J. Sakai. He was extremely influential for MAC core ideology and, as we enter our fourth anniversary, and a Zionist Entity demonstrates its usefulness, we believe he needs to be highlighted.

We must be clear about who J. Sakai isn’t before clarifiying who he is : J. Sakai is not a postcolonial intellectual criticizing Marxism for being the racist (or Eurocentric) product of a white male. This argument was never the point of Sakai’s thesis, and was mostly an invention of post-1991 academics to justify the end of History and the absence of science.

A long article should be produced about the accusation of Eurocentrism, but a point I like to highlight about Marx’s position is his opinion on Cromwell, the Robespierre and Napoléon of the English revolution :

In 1652 the 2nd Complete Conquest of Ireland completed. Division of spoils: the Government itself, the “ adventurers” who had lent £360,000 for the 11 years of war, the officers and soldiers, by the Acts of the English Parliament, 12 August, 1652, and 26 September, l653. Smite the Amalekites of the Irish Nation hip and thigh, and replant the re-devastated land with new colonies of brand-new Puritan English.-Bloodshed, devastation, depopulation of entire counties, removal of their inhabitants to other regions, sale of many Irish into slavery in the West Indies. By engaging in the conquest of Ireland, Cromwell threw the English Republic out the window.
The 80,000 Protestants’ massacre of 1641. The Irish Catholics are here in the same position as the Commune de Paris. The Versaillais massacred 30,000 Communards and called that the horrors of the Commune. The English Protestants under Cromwell massacred at least 30,000 Irish and to cover their brutality, invented the tale that this was to avenge 30,000 Protestants murdered by the Irish Catholics…
Ireland was Catholic, Protestant England Republican, therefore Ireland-English Vendée. There is however this little difference that the French Revolution intended to give the land to the people, the English Commonwealth intended, in Ireland, to take the land from the people.

Karl Bottigheimer explains well what he saw in Ireland.

Of the 1,533 adventurers it is possible to attribute a geographical location to all but 202. Of the 1,331 who can be classified, 750 prove to have been from London… The remaining adventure money came from all over the country… the West Country as a whole generated an amount of capital and a number of investors unmatched by any other provincial area. The West had long been identified with Irish colonization, particularly with the plantation of Ulster in the late sixteenth century, but this is not necessarily an adequate explanation of the enthusiasm of Devon. In addition, the phenomenon of small investment is nowhere more visible than in Exeter… The adventure in Exeter was therefore markedly different from the adventure in London, where it was largely the province of very rich men. This was the main difference between revolutionary socialism represented by Marx and revisionist social-fascism represented by many of his disciples. Sakai calls himself a disciple of Marx, and follows Marxism.

Sakai is also not a caricatural Third-worldist who believes no revolutionary subject exist, no revolutionary project can be provided to the masses because they are helped by Imperialism. This is the complete reverse. J. Sakai studies if there is a revolutionary subject in America. This is why he is ideologically of Maoist origin, a doctrine that always tried to build on new analysis of modern Imperialism. After all, it is the doctrine that managed to construct concepts such as Social-Imperialism, trying to decipher Soviet Union’s chauvinist policy, the capitalist character of most third-world “socialist” states, etc.

So who is he? He tries to study America, including the birth of white nation, and wants to answer this simple question : is there a proletariat in “ American” formation? How did “ American “ formation construct itself? He is clear, building on Lenin’s prediction

On the one hand, there is the tendency of the bourgeoisie and the opportunists to convert a handful of very rich and privileged nations into ‘eternal’ parasites on the body of the rest of mankind, to ‘rest on the laurels’ of the exploitation of Negroes, Indians, etc., keeping them in subjection with the aid of excellent weapons of extermination provided by modern militarism. On the other hand, there is the tendency of the masses, who are more oppressed than before and who bear the whole brunt of imperialist wars, to cast off this yoke and to overthrow the bourgeoisie. It is in the struggle between these two tendencies that the history of the labour movement will now inevitably develop.

Sakai tries, looking after this Leninist analysis, to come up with how a settler-colonial society was built on. He concludes in these four principles of settler-colonialism : (1)A study of approximately 10,000 settlers who left Bristol between 1654 and 1685 found that less than 15% came from the proletariat. (2)This reflects the core appeal of colonial North America for Europeans: the dream of each man becoming a petty lord over his own land. (3) Thus, the American tradition of individualism and egalitarianism was rooted in the toxic idea of equal privileges among European conquerors (4) Genocide and slavery were not incidental, but essential to capitalist expansion — exploitation shaped the very structure of Euro-American society.

All sections of white settler society — even the artisan, worker, and farmer — were totally dependent upon Afrikan slave labor: the fisherman whose low-grade,”refuse fish” was dried and sold as slave meal in the Indies; the New York farmer who found his market for surpluses in the Southern plantations.

Again, you can read about the example of Ireland we already talk about. The hope of becoming a petty bourgeois and the owner of a land is the material interest of a settler, and what makes a settler a settler.

It was [land] alone that drew so many Europeans to colonial North Amerika: the dream in the settler mind of each man becoming a petty lord of his own land. Thus, the tradition of individualism and egalitarianism in Amerika was rooted in the… concept of equal privileges for a new nation’ of European conquerors.

Sakai doesn’t just criticize imperialism for its immediate material interests, such as the simplistic idea that the Iraq War was launched solely for oil. He openly mocks this naive and reductionist view, supported by activists who confuse root causes with media justifications.

At the heart of his analysis, Sakai argues that the wars waged against countries like Iraq, Libya, and Syria are not primarily aimed at neocolonial exploitation. These nations had already been peacefully integrated into the imperial world order since the 1980s. They were liberalizing their economies, opening their markets, and collaborating with Western powers. For example: Libya, well before its bombing, had embarked on a process of liberalization, was collaborating with Western companies, and was already delivering its oil. Bashar al-Assad’s Syria also sought to draw closer to the capitalist powers, breaking with his father’s more resolute independence stance. Even the Taliban, before their overthrow in 2001, had expressed their willingness to negotiate the exploitation of hydrocarbons through special economic zones.

These regimes therefore posed a problem not of access to resources, but of political existence. What the American empire cannot tolerate are nation-states that, even when cooperating, retain an autonomous capacity for negotiation, a potential sovereignty. The objective is not simply to “take the oil,” but to break the state, deconstruct society, and reconfigure the geopolitical space according to the needs of imperialism.

These are intra-capitalist wars of local capitalist insurgencies trying to win back control of “their” nations from the Great Powers.

The popular political struggles of settler Amerika — the most important being the 1775–83 War of Independence — gave us the first experience of alliances between Euro-Amerikan dissenters and oppressed peoples. What was most basic in these alliances was their purely tactical nature. Not unity, but the momentary convergence of the fundamentally differing interests of some oppressors and some of the oppressed. After all, the national division between settler citizens of emerging Amerika and their colonial Afrikan subjects was enormous — while the distance between the interests of Indian nations and that of the settler nation built on their destruction was hardly any less.
